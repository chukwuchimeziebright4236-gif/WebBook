Meet Bashir Usman: The Lone Lawmaker Who Voted Against The State Police Bill

ABUJA, NIGERIA — As the National Assembly edges closer to altering the 1999 Constitution to decentralize internal security, attention has shifted to Hon. Bashir Usman, the single dissenting voice in a historic House of Representatives vote.

The landmark piece of legislation, officially titled “A Bill for an Act to Alter the Constitution of the Federal Republic of Nigeria, 1999 to Provide for the Establishment of State Police and for Related Matters (Sixth Alteration) Bill, 2026,” scaled a massive hurdle when 289 out of 290 lawmakers present voted firmly in favor of its passage.

However, Honorable Bashir Usman, who represents the Birnin-Gwari/Giwa Federal Constituency of Kaduna State, boldly stood alone to cast the sole "Nay" vote against the security blueprint, sparking intense national conversation regarding legislative due process.

A Dissent Rooted in Procedure, Not Policy

Clarifying his controversial stance on the floor of the House and in subsequent briefs, Hon. Usman explained that his objection was not a rejection of the core merits of decentralizing Nigeria's policing structure. Instead, his lone stance was a matter of strict legislative principle regarding a severe procedural error.

According to Usman, the report from the House Committee on Constitution Review was distributed to members too late, denying lawmakers the requisite time needed to properly dissect, scrutinize, and weigh the vast economic and operational implications of the clauses before voting.

"We are talking about a major constitutional alteration affecting national security architecture. Members must be given ample time to review the fine details. Passing legislation under rushed procedural windows risks leaving critical loopholes unaddressed," a source familiar with his position noted.

Economic & Political Safeguards Under the Radar

The State Police Bill outlines massive shifts in sub-national administration, a core focus for the business and economic community. The new provisions alter Section 214 of the Constitution, introducing a dual-layered system comprising the Federal Police and individual State Police formations.

Crucially for state budgets, the bill stipulates that no state police force can commence operations unless it is backed by legislation from its respective State House of Assembly and certified as meeting national minimum benchmarks. Furthermore, the bill introduces safeguards allowing the Federal Police to step in only under a total breakdown of law and order or via a direct request from a governor.

What Comes Next?

Despite Hon. Usman’s lone resistance, the momentum behind the security reform remains strong. Following the overwhelming House approval, the bill was formally transmitted to the Senate, where it has continued to receive accelerated, high-priority consideration backed by executive communication from the presidency.

To fully become part of Nigeria’s supreme law, the bill must achieve concurrence in both chambers of the National Assembly and secure ratification by at least two-thirds (24 out of 36) of the State Houses of Assembly before landing on the desk of President Bola Tinubu for final presidential assent.
